When the cat’s away
The relationship between Russia and Georgia has been tense ever since the fall of the Soviet Union and Russia’s annexation of Abkhazia and South Ossetia, two autonomous regions which Georgia and most of the world recognise as part of Georgia but which Russia and a few allies call independent states. In August 2008, after George W. Bush announced his support for Georgia joining NATO, tension boiled over and resulted in a bloody 5-day war between Russia and Georgia. By 2021, Georgia was still not a member of NATO and Russia still controlled Abkhazia and South Ossetia.

In 2014, a long-running conflict between the predominantly Sunni government of Yemen and rebels belonging to the mainly Shia Houthi movement—with links to Iran—erupted into a civil war. After the Houthis captured the capital, the conflict turned into another front in the contest for regional dominance between Iran and Saudi Arabia and another example of the US' inability to restrain military adventurism in the Middle East. The ousted government appealed to the Saudis, who intervened militarily on its side, leading a coalition of eight other countries. Iran, according to a UN report, backed the Houthis with money, training and arms. The US was also caught up in the fighting after one of its warships was attacked by missiles fired, the US said, by the Houthis. Despite ceasefires in 2016 and 2020, the war ground slowly on, leading to a stalemate and what, in 2020, UNICEF called “the largest humanitarian crisis in the world”.

In 2013, Ukraine’s pro-Russian government suspended the signing of an association agreement with the European Union, sparking mass protests by the pro-European Maidan movement, the Ukrainian revolution and the overthrow of Ukraine’s pro-Russian government. In 2014, Russia took advantage of the internal conflict to impose itself in this part of the former Soviet Union, calculating that the US would not be able to deter it. First, it annexed Crimea, a peninsula in the Black Sea. Protestors in Crimea, backed by disguised Russian soldiers, rejected the new Ukrainian government, seized the local parliament building and organised a referendum, in which, Russia claimed, Crimeans voted overwhelmingly to join Russia. Soon after pro-Russian rebels seized territory in eastern Ukraine with the help of the Russian army, which led to brutal fighting and a humanitarian crisis.

In August 2012, Barack Obama gave a warning to Syria's president that the use of chemical weapons by the Assad government in the country's civil war would cross a “red line”. One year later, opposition-held areas near Damascus were struck by missiles containing sarin gas. Hundreds died. Obama asked for a Congressional resolution to authorise military action in Syria but, against a background of popular opposition to such action, the resolution was never voted on. Instead, Russia negotiated a deal under which Syria agreed to destroy its chemical-weapons stockpile and to join the Chemical Weapons Convention.
